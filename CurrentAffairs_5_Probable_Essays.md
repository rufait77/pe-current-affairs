## Essay 1 — Bangladesh's Democratic Transition: From the July Uprising to the 13th Parliamentary Election

**Introduction.** Democratic transitions are the defining inflection points in the evolution of any modern republic. Bangladesh's transition from the July 2024 uprising, through an eighteen-month interim government, to the 13th parliamentary election on 12 February 2026 is the single most consequential political episode in the country's recent history. This essay examines how the interim administration safeguarded constitutional continuity and how the February 2026 election restored electoral legitimacy, arguing that the transition has redefined the operating compact between the state and its uniformed services.

**The Interim Government's Stewardship.** The interim government led by Chief Adviser Dr Muhammad Yunus succeeded in holding the political middle ground through a highly volatile period. It issued the July Charter Implementation Order on 7 November 2025, promulgated the International Crimes Tribunal (Third Amendment) Ordinance 2025, reconstituted the 30th Election Commission, deregistered the Awami League, and delivered the election on its announced date of 12 February 2026 — all while absorbing shocks such as the 18 December 2025 assassination of Osman Hadi and the associated riots. These actions preserved the constitutional spine at a moment when drift could easily have hardened into crisis. The interim period therefore demonstrated that a non-partisan caretaker mechanism, supported by disciplined armed forces, can guide a republic through transition without constitutional rupture.

**Restoration of Electoral Legitimacy.** The election itself reset the country's political compass with a decisive popular mandate. The Bangladesh Nationalist Party secured 209 of 297 declared seats — a two-thirds majority — Jamaat-e-Islami emerged as the principal opposition with 68 seats, and Tarique Rahman was sworn in as the 11th Prime Minister on 17 February 2026 at the South Plaza of the Jatiya Sangsad Bhaban, with Justice Zubayer Rahman Chowdhury already installed as the 26th Chief Justice on 28 December 2025. The new cabinet — 25 ministers and 24 state ministers, with the Prime Minister personally retaining the Defence Ministry and Armed Forces Division — signals concentrated executive authority within a constitutional frame. The outcome thus restores civilian primacy while requiring the services to remain apolitical, professional, and focused on their constitutional role.

**Conclusion.** In sum, the combination of the July uprising, the Yunus interim government, and the February 2026 election shows that institutional resilience is ultimately a product of disciplined civilian leadership and equally disciplined military restraint. In my view, it is precisely the professional neutrality of the Armed Forces during this cycle that allowed the transition to succeed, and preserving that neutrality must remain the cornerstone of our institutional ethos.

## Essay 2 — LDC Graduation in 2026: Opportunities, Risks and the Way Forward for Bangladesh

**Introduction.** In a middle-income state, economic sovereignty is now the single most direct determinant of national security. Bangladesh's scheduled graduation from Least Developed Country (LDC) status in November 2026 marks the largest structural shift in the country's external economic framework in a generation. This essay analyses the specific risks that graduation poses to the export sector and the policy measures the government has deployed to cushion the transition, arguing that diversification and a first generation of free-trade agreements together form the most credible bridge to the post-LDC era.

**The Export Sector at Greatest Risk.** The risks of graduation fall most heavily on the garments-led export economy. Loss of duty-free access under schemes such as the EU's Everything But Arms and preferential access in the UK, Canada and Australia will raise effective tariffs on RMG by roughly 8–12% overnight; Bangladesh's export base of 172 categories of agricultural and processed goods in FY 2024-25 is still narrow; and inflation has risen sequentially to 8.49% (December 2025), 8.58% (January 2026) and 9.13% (February 2026), the highest reading since April 2025. These figures indicate that consumer affordability is already stretched, so a tariff shock on exports would feed back through employment, remittance-adjacent demand and foreign-exchange earnings at a time when gross reserves — though at a three-year high of $29.86 billion (IMF BPM-6, 22 December 2025) — remain fragile. The export sector is therefore not merely the engine but also the most exposed cylinder of the national economy.

**A Multi-Pronged Adjustment Strategy.** A multi-pronged adjustment has, however, been set in motion across trade policy, macro-stabilisation and industrial diversification. Bangladesh signed its first Economic Partnership Agreement (EPA) with Japan; a prospective free-trade agreement with China is under negotiation on the back of a 98% zero-tariff offer; the IMF's fifth review under the $5.5 billion ECF/EFF/RSF programme was approved in November 2025, and the sixth tranche is due after the April 2026 mission; and remittance inflows have been robust, with January 2026 posting $3.17 billion — the third-highest month on record. Taken together, these measures stabilise external balances, lock in preferential access with new partners, and create policy space to invest in skill-intensive sectors such as ICT, pharmaceuticals and light engineering — Bangladesh's Priyoshop winning the South Asia ICT Awards 2025 is an early signal of this shift. The graduation cliff can therefore be transformed into a ramp, provided that execution keeps pace with the November 2026 deadline.

**Conclusion.** Taken together, LDC graduation is less a loss of concessions than a forced evolution toward a more self-reliant, diversified and competitive economy. In my assessment, Bangladesh's post-2026 security will depend less on preserving old preferences than on completing the EPA and FTA pipeline, deepening domestic value addition, and treating economic resilience as an explicit component of the national-security doctrine.

## Essay 3 — The Rohingya Crisis: A Protracted Humanitarian Emergency and Bangladesh's National Security

**Introduction.** Protracted displacement along a state's frontier is as much a national-security problem as a humanitarian one. The Rohingya crisis — now in its ninth year since the 2017 exodus and newly aggravated by fresh inflows from Myanmar — has become Bangladesh's longest-running frontier-security challenge. This essay examines the scale and security dimension of the crisis and evaluates the operational-diplomatic tracks available, arguing that border management, camp governance and a multilateral repatriation framework must advance in parallel.

**Scale and a Worsening Myanmar Backdrop.** The sheer scale of the caseload and the deteriorating Myanmar backdrop now define the operating environment. As of 31 January 2026, Bangladesh hosts 1,182,755 Rohingya from 245,998 families; approximately 150,000 new arrivals were recorded during 2024-25; the Myanmar junta controls fewer than 40% of the country's 330 townships; and the airstrike on Bhamo Township in Kachin State on 22 January 2026 killed up to 50 civilians. These numbers indicate that short-term repatriation is not operationally feasible, while the intensifying volatility inside Myanmar keeps inflow pressure high and raises the risk of armed-actor spillover into the Cox's Bazar camps. Bangladesh is therefore carrying a protracted population at the precise moment when the regional environment has actually worsened.

**A Layered Diplomatic-Operational Response.** A layered response across diplomacy, camp governance and border operations is the only sustainable path forward. The 2025-2026 Joint Response Plan — administered by UNHCR and IOM — anchors the humanitarian track; ASEAN's Philippines-chaired 2026 agenda (with the 48th Summit in Cebu on 8–9 May 2026) and China's continuing trilateral mediation create diplomatic leverage; and domestic decisions such as opening new embassies in Dublin and Buenos Aires, alongside the resumption of bilateral talks with India on 7–9 April 2026, broaden the coalition for repatriation. At the security level, Border Guard Bangladesh deployment, biometric registration, and continuous counter-narcotics and counter-trafficking operations inside Ukhiya and Teknaf remain the decisive first line, complemented by the Armed Forces' logistical enabling role. The crisis therefore cannot be resolved by any single ministry or mission; it requires synchronous action along humanitarian, diplomatic and military axes.

**Conclusion.** In sum, the Rohingya crisis today is a multi-dimensional protracted emergency that simultaneously tests Bangladesh's frontier security, diplomatic stamina and humanitarian capacity. My view is that the long-term cost of continued inaction far exceeds the cost of building a credible multilateral pressure track on Myanmar, and the Armed Forces occupy a unique enabling role in making that pressure operationally credible.

## Essay 4 — Self-Reliance in Defence: The Military Economic Zone and Indigenous Capability

**Introduction.** Modern warfare since 2022 has made clear that the side which can sustain its own supply of unmanned systems and precision munitions retains the operational initiative. Bangladesh's emerging drive toward defence self-reliance — crystallised in the new Military Economic Zone at Mirsarai and the announced G2G drone-manufacturing agreement with China — represents a doctrinal shift rather than a routine procurement decision. This essay analyses the strategic rationale for indigenous defence capability and examines the institutional preconditions for its success, arguing that hardware acquisition must be matched by investment in human capital and the surrounding industrial ecosystem.

**Lessons from Contemporary Warfare.** The rationale rests on hard lessons drawn from the ongoing reshaping of warfare by drones and precision weapons. The Russia-Ukraine war and the 2026 Iran war — in which Operation Midnight Hammer / Roaring Lion delivered nearly 900 strikes in the first 12 hours from 28 February 2026 — have underlined the centrality of UAV platforms such as the MQ-9 Reaper and the Bayraktar family (TB2, Akıncı, KIZILELMA), and Bangladesh's G2G agreement with China for domestic drone manufacture fits this global pattern. Indigenous production insulates Bangladesh from sanctions, export controls and wartime supply shocks that can cripple a force dependent on foreign OEMs, while also generating industrial spill-overs in dual-use electronics, composites and software. Self-reliance in drones is therefore both a capability upgrade and an act of strategic sovereignty.

**Capability as an Ecosystem, Not a Factory.** Capability, however, is never a factory alone — it is an ecosystem of people, processes and procurement discipline. The Mirsarai Military Economic Zone, Bangladesh Ordnance Factories, the Defence Services Command and Staff College and the public-university engineering base together form the spine; but the demand side will depend on a credible procurement roadmap under a refreshed Forces Goal 2030, and on uniformed officers trained in systems engineering, cyber warfare and integrated air-defence. Without a standing requirements pipeline and a deep bench of technically literate officers, domestic production risks remaining assembly rather than true manufacture. The measure of success will therefore be the depth of domestic R&D and the number of Bangladeshi officers, scientists and engineers who can design, field-test and sustain indigenous systems end-to-end.

**Conclusion.** Overall, the Military Economic Zone and the drone G2G agreement together constitute the most ambitious defence-industrial move by Bangladesh in a decade, but their strategic payoff will be decided by human capital, not by machinery alone. In my opinion, every officer of our generation has a direct professional stake in this transition, and our ability to operate tomorrow's systems will depend squarely on the technical literacy we build today.

## Essay 5 — Bangladesh in a Shifting Indo-Pacific: Balancing India, China and the United States

**Introduction.** The centre of global economic and strategic gravity has shifted decisively to the Indo-Pacific. Bangladesh — situated at the head of the Bay of Bengal, sharing land borders with India and Myanmar and commanding maritime access to the Indian Ocean — is now an indispensable node within the India-China-United States triangle. This essay examines how Bangladesh's relationships with each of the three powers have been recalibrated in 2025-2026, arguing that strategic autonomy anchored in a credible Blue Economy posture is the only sustainable response.

**Recalibration of the Three Bilateral Tracks.** The bilateral tracks with Delhi, Beijing and Washington have each been visibly re-set within the space of a single year. The April 2026 Delhi talks led by External Affairs Minister S. Jaishankar — hosting Foreign Minister Khalilur Rahman and the Prime Minister's Adviser Humayun Kobir on 7–9 April 2026 — reopened normalisation, including the India-Bangladesh Friendship Pipeline (180,000 tonnes of diesel per year), the renewed extradition request for Sheikh Hasina, and the negotiation of a successor to the 1996 Ganga Waters Treaty expiring in December 2026; China marked 50 years of diplomatic ties with Bangladesh in 2025 through a combined $40 billion BRI and joint-venture envelope and a 98% zero-tariff offer ahead of a possible 2026 FTA; and on 9 January 2026 Brent T. Christensen took oath as the 18th United States Ambassador to Bangladesh. Each track serves a different national interest — India for contiguity and water, China for infrastructure and finance, the United States for markets and technology — and all three powers are now willing to invest political capital rather than let the relationship drift. Bangladesh's diplomatic bandwidth is therefore being tested simultaneously along three fronts.

**The Bay of Bengal as a First-Tier National Interest.** The re-militarisation of the Bay of Bengal demands that Bangladesh treat maritime security as a first-tier national interest. India is reportedly planning a naval detachment at Haldia in West Bengal explicitly to monitor Bangladesh and China; Chinese BRI port and infrastructure investments continue to widen; and the United States under the Trump administration has paired tariff escalation (25% autos, 50% steel and aluminium, 47.5% on Chinese autos) with renewed diplomatic visibility in Dhaka. In this environment, Bangladesh Navy modernisation, UAV-enabled maritime ISR, and Blue Economy projects — from offshore energy to the Matarbari deep-sea port whose 760-metre terminal contract was signed in April 2026 — are no longer economic optional extras; they are instruments of strategic autonomy. Maritime capability is therefore the most practical and observable expression of Bangladesh's non-aligned ambition.

**Conclusion.** In sum, the new Indo-Pacific environment has multiplied Bangladesh's opportunities and obligations in parallel, forcing a more agile and maritime-facing foreign policy than the country has ever attempted. My view is that strategic autonomy is the posture most consistent with Bangladesh's history and interests, and our Armed Forces must be resourced and trained to underwrite that autonomy at sea, in the air, and across the electromagnetic spectrum.
